## Abstract

Paper 1 introduced VERDICT WEIGHT and its eight-stream composition. Paper 2 presented the empirical case. This paper presents the formal architectural treatment: the failure-class taxonomy the framework targets, the per-stream coverage argument, the necessity argument grounded in ablation results, and the soundness of the composition rule under the documented threat model.

The central result is a **completeness theorem** relative to a documented failure-class taxonomy F1&ndash;F8: every failure class in the taxonomy is detected, suppressed, or surfaced by at least one stream, and no smaller subset of the streams retains that coverage. The composition rule is shown to route each detection to its appropriate downstream effect (graded contribution, abstention, or veto) under a priority ordering that makes the safe failure mode the default.

The paper treats three caveats explicitly: the threat-model dependence of the completeness claim, the absence of an adaptive-adversary security guarantee for the Curveball-detection stream, and the in-distribution nature of the calibration guarantee. These caveats are part of the result, not concessions to it &mdash; the framework's positioning depends on stating precisely what is and is not claimed.

The architecture paper is intended as the canonical reference for engineers and reviewers evaluating VERDICT WEIGHT for adoption in high-stakes deployments.

## Why three papers and not one

The work is split across three papers because the audiences and the claim types are different:

| Paper | Audience | Claim type |
|---|---|---|
| **Paper 1** | Practitioners; framework users | Existence and design rationale |
| **Paper 2** | Empirical researchers; reviewers | Quantitative performance and reliability |
| **Paper 3** | Architects; formal-methods readers | Coverage, necessity, soundness |

Combining them would produce a paper too long for any single venue and too unfocused for any single audience. The three-paper structure reflects the practical reality of presenting a framework that is simultaneously an engineering artifact, an empirical study, and a formal architecture.

## How this paper relates to deployment

The completeness argument is what supports the framework's defense and regulated-industry positioning. A coverage claim relative to a documented threat model is the strongest claim a confidence framework can make without crossing into unfalsifiable territory. Papers 1 and 2 are necessary; Paper 3 is what makes the case to a security-architecture audience that the framework is the right shape for the job.
